Background
Why Is This Problem Important?
Urban Mobility and Infrastructure Planning
Morning rush hour is the single most stressful period for transit systems. Identifying where surges will concentrate, not just that they will happen, gives city planners and the MTA actionable intelligence for deploying staff, adjusting service frequency, or redesigning platform flow before congestion becomes a safety issue.
Beyond Commuting: The "Third Place" Hypothesis
The inclusion of café and third-place density is the intellectually interesting bet in this research. The implicit hypothesis is that subway hotspots are not purely commuter-driven; they are also shaped by where people choose to linger before work. If the Coffee Contribution Index confirms this, it reframes hotspots as a behavioral and urban design phenomenon rather than just a transit scheduling problem. That has real implications for how cities zone commercial activity near transit and how businesses choose locations.
Methodological Value
Most ridership forecasting treats stations as independent time series. Building a spatial graph that encodes proximity and shared line connectivity lets the model propagate information between stations, recognizing that a surge at 72nd St likely signals what is coming at 59th St. This mirrors how congestion actually moves through a network, making predictions structurally more honest.
Disentangling Causes, Not Just Predicting Outcomes
The multi-branch architecture with structured feature perturbation is designed to answer why a hotspot forms, not just whether it will. That is a much harder and more valuable question, since policymakers need causal levers, not just forecasts.
The News and Context Embedding Angle
Incorporating daily Manhattan news headlines via Sentence-BERT is a novel signal. It captures things like major events, weather warnings, or transit disruptions that show up in public discourse before they appear in ridership data, essentially giving the model a soft real-world awareness layer.
The core reason this problem matters is that it connects transit operations, urban behavioral economics, and causal machine learning in a way that could directly inform how dense cities manage their most congested daily moments.

Literature Review
Spatio-Temporal Graph Networks for Transit Demand
The dominant model in this area involves encoding transit stations as graph nodes and using graph neural networks to capture spatial dependencies alongside temporal models. Researchers have constructed graphs from physical connections between stations, passenger flow sequence similarities, and functional correlations to learn effective spatio-temporal representations (Lu et al., 2025). The combination of GCNs or GATs with recurrent architectures like GRU is now well-established. One prominent approach proposes using dilated convolutions to extract multi-dimensional temporal dependencies across multiple channels, then applying GRU to enhance temporal feature extraction, evaluated on metro ridership data from two Chinese cities (Li et al., 2025).
There has also been pushback on whether graph structure always helps. Some recent work argues that GCN-based models fail to effectively capture global spatial correlations, and that Transformer-based models can lose temporal information due to the permutation-invariant properties of self-attention, motivating hybrid architectures that combine both (Yang et al., 2024). The LSTM-only baseline in this paper directly engages with that question, isolating the contribution of spatial message passing to hotspot prediction.
Incorporating External Features: POI, Weather, and Land Use
Bringing in non-ridership signals has become standard practice in the field, though the specific combination varies. Point of Interest data is widely used to capture land use and the distribution of functional areas around subway stations, with stations near residential neighborhoods and business districts tending to show higher commuter flows; weather is also incorporated as a standard influence on travel patterns (Hu et al., 2024). Work like ST-MFGCRN explicitly fuses multiple regional features including land use, sociodemographics, and POI distribution through a sentinel attention mechanism that allows each region to take partial attention depending on feature relevance (Han et al., 2024).
The café density branch in this model is a more specific and theoretically motivated version of the POI signal common in these models. Rather than treating all nearby amenities as equivalent, it isolates third-place density as a behavioral driver of pre-commute lingering and tests that hypothesis through structured attribution.
Text and Event-Based Signals
Using natural language data in transit prediction is less common but has real precedent. Earlier approaches applied topic modeling (LDA) to event descriptions and combined them with transit smart card data, or used pretrained word embeddings with convolutional layers, though these methods sometimes struggled to capture full paragraph semantics (Liang et al., 2023). More recently, research has explored large language models for predicting mobility under public events, motivated by the fact that event organizers advertise on ticketing websites and social media with rich textual descriptions that earlier methods largely ignored (Liang et al., 2023).
One earlier study proposed a bi-directional LSTM model that combines information extracted from tweets with traffic and weather data, specifically to handle non-recurring events that degrade prediction accuracy in purely data-driven models (Essien et al., 2021). The use of Sentence-BERT on daily Manhattan news headlines in this work extends that line of research from event-specific triggers toward ambient urban context, an input type that remains largely unexplored.
Urban Hotspot Detection
The broader hotspot detection literature mostly focuses on clustering mobility or trajectory data to find high-activity zones, rather than predicting them. Urban hotspot area detection has been approached as a spatial clustering problem applied to taxi trajectory data, with detected hotspot areas used as reference information for traffic guidance and public facility layout (Yu et al., 2021). Framing hotspot status as a classification target for a neural model moves the problem from retrospective identification to forward-looking prediction, which is the operationally useful formulation for transit management.
What This Work Adds
Taken together, prior work has established strong baselines for spatio-temporal graph prediction and multi-feature fusion, but has not combined these tools in the specific context of morning surge classification in a dense North American subway network, nor attempted to disentangle the contribution of third-place commercial density from commuter and environmental signals. The Coffee Contribution Index, computed through branch-level attribution and structured feature perturbation, does not have a close analog in the existing literature.
